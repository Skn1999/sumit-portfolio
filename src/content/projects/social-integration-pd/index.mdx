## Overview

You move into a new apartment. There's a courtyard downstairs, a couple of benches, a patch of grass. On paper it's a "common area." In practice, nobody uses it. The kids from the subsidized units play on one side. The families who bought at market price stay on the other. Everyone nods politely and goes inside.

That gap, between a space that *exists* and a space that *belongs to people*, is what this project is about.

Mesa Te Club is a participatory design framework that turns awkward shared spaces in Chilean social housing into places people actually want to be in. Not by handing residents a finished blueprint, but by sitting them down with tea, snacks, and a set of playful tools and letting them design the spaces themselves, alongside developers, municipalities, and the housing ministry.

> _I led workshop design, research synthesis, and co-design tool creation across the full DS19 project timeline._

The idea is reducing the socio-economic segregation that happens between people living in the same building.

### 2. The reframe that unlocked everything

The original brief was broad: "design a participatory process for DS19 common areas." That could mean anything. Workshops, surveys, apps, committees.

But when I looked at what already existed â€” the Mesa Te Club tea gatherings â€” I realized I didn't need to invent a new format. I needed to **redesign an existing one**.

> Mesa Te Club was already a ritual. It had trust, familiarity, a name. All it needed was design power.

## What I designed

### A participation ladder, disguised as tea time

The framework has six activities, each designed for a different moment and a different kind of input. Together, they form a ladder â€” from early idea crowdsourcing to post-occupancy learning.

### Tree of Dreams â€” surfacing what people hope for

Before any design happens, we need to know what people care about. "Tree of Dreams" is a low-barrier activity. Residents attach notes to a physical tree in the sales room or contribute digitally, sharing what they wish for in their future home and shared spaces.

Safety. Nature. A place for kids to play. Somewhere to sit and read. A garden.

The goal here isn't consensus but to understand the variety of needs of the residents.

### Build-A-Space Lab â€” translating dreams into requirements

Once we have a rich picture of needs, I designed a 2.5-hour expert workshop. Architects, planners, municipal social workers, and developers sit together and work through a **Common Area Canvas** I created.

They collaboratively define components (play, sport, relax, nature, culture), constraints (cost, maintenance, geography), and how each element supports social integration. The output is a set of clear requirements but grounded in feasibility.

> This is where dreams meet reality. But the point isn't to kill dreams â€” it's to make them buildable.

### Playing Sims â€” residents design the layout

This is the heart of the project. A 2-hour Mesa Te Club session where groups of residents receive a blank plan of their future building with highlighted common areas, and a set of **pre-priced modular pieces** â€” playground, benches, gardens, courts, a barbecue area.

Each group negotiates how to fill the plan within spatial and budget constraints. Then everyone votes on their favorite configuration using stickers. For example, if someone votes for a bigger playground, they have to vote against a garden or a seating area. The trade-offs become visible, and priorities become shared.

### Design Reveal â€” closing the loop

After integrating expert and resident inputs, I designed a "Design Reveal" moment. Through email, website, and WhatsApp, residents see the final chosen common areas, what was possible, what wasn't, and why.

This step is about managing expectations and reinforcing that **participation led to concrete outcomes**. People gave input. The design reflects it. Here's the proof.

### "Never Have I Ever" â€” learning after move-in

Months after delivery, residents, administrators and developers sit in the built common areas and play a custom version of "Never Have I Ever." Cards prompt stories about safety, events, maintenance, and inclusivity. Designated note-takers capture insights.

The session closes the feedback loop of "what we designed, how it's actually being used, and what the next building should do differently". 

> The most important design moment isn't the workshop. It's six months after everyone moves in.
